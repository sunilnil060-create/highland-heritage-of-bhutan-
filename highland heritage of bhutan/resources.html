✦ Field Notes

A Timeline of the Archive

Key milestones from the Highland Heritage Project's field seasons — documenting when and how these stories were recorded, and with whom.

June 2023

Preliminary Fieldwork — Haa Valley

The project team conducted initial scoping visits to Haa Dzongkhag, meeting with village elders, local government officials, and the Haa Dzongkhag Agriculture Office to identify community partners and sacred sites for documentation.

Haa Scoping

September–October 2023

Royal Highland Festival — Laya Documentation

A team of RTC students and faculty attended the Royal Highland Festival in Laya during September 2023. Film footage, photographic documentation, and oral history interviews were conducted with participating herding families, festival organisers, and yak breeders.

Laya Film Festival

December 2023

Sephu Fieldwork — Tshonapata Sacred Lake

Three weeks of ethnographic fieldwork in Sephu, Wangdue Phodrang, focused on the sacred lake of Tshonapata. The team recorded oral histories from elder community members describing the lake's cosmological significance and the lu (water spirit) rituals performed seasonally. Community consent and ethics protocols were observed throughout.

Sephu Vignette Oral History

March 2024

Thimphu Sacred Rituals Documentation

Photo essay fieldwork documenting the sacred ritual practices of Thimphu — including the Tsechu mask dance festival, butter lamp offerings, and monastery life. Interviews were conducted with monks and lay practitioners about the intersection of daily life and Buddhist cosmology.

Thimphu Photo Essay Ritual

June–July 2024

Haa Cliffside Temple & Transhumance Routes

Return visit to Haa to document the seasonal transhumance routes used by yak herding families migrating between lowland winter pastures and high-altitude summer grazing grounds. The documentary film "Voices of the Herders" was completed during this season, and the Jungney cliffside temple was photographed in detail.

Haa Film Transhumance

January 2025

Archive Launch — Highland Heritage Portal Goes Live

The Highland Heritage of Bhutan digital archive was officially launched at Royal Thimphu College with a community screening and sharing event attended by participants from all three fieldwork locations. The portal is now publicly accessible for academic and educational use.

Launch All Locations

✦ Glossary

Dzongkha & Cultural Terms

Key terms from Dzongkha, Tibetan, and Bhutanese cultural tradition that appear throughout this archive — with phonetic guides and contextual definitions.

Tshona

tsho-na

A sacred lake or high-altitude body of water, often considered the dwelling place of water spirits (lu) and protected from human interference by both community law and Buddhist practice.

Lu

lü

Water spirits or nāgas in Bhutanese cosmology — powerful non-human entities believed to inhabit rivers, lakes, and underground streams. Offerings and rituals are performed seasonally to maintain their favour and prevent ecological harm.

Tsechu

tse-chu

An annual religious festival celebrated on the tenth day of the lunar month — featuring masked dances (Cham), sacred music, and large thangka displays. Tsechus are both spiritual events and important social gatherings for highland communities.

Dzongkhag

dzong-kag

A district — Bhutan's primary administrative division, of which there are twenty. Each Dzongkhag is anchored by a dzong (fortress-monastery) and governed by a Dzongda (district governor).

Yak

yak / nor

The large bovid (Bos grunniens) central to Bhutanese highland life. Known locally as "nor" in Dzongkha, the yak provides milk, butter, meat, wool, and transport — and holds deep spiritual and cultural significance in highland communities.

Cham

cham

Sacred masked dance performed by monks during Tsechu festivals. Each dance enacts episodes from Buddhist cosmology — typically the subjugation of evil spirits and the triumph of dharma — and serves as a visual teaching for lay communities.

Lungta

lung-ta

Literally "wind horse" — the prayer flags strung across mountain passes, ridgelines, and bridges throughout Bhutan. Each flag carries prayers and mantras which are "carried" on the wind to benefit all beings in the surrounding landscape.

Terma

ter-ma

Hidden treasures — sacred texts, objects, or teachings concealed by Guru Rinpoche (Padmasambhava) and other masters throughout the Himalayan landscape, to be discovered by destined treasure-revealers (tertöns) in future generations.

Driglam Namzha

drig-lam nam-zha

Bhutan's official code of etiquette and traditional dress — governing behaviour in public spaces, dzongs, and official settings. It mandates the wearing of the Gho (men's robe) and Kira (women's dress) in formal contexts.

Lhakhang

lha-kang

A temple or shrine — often a small community place of worship distinct from a monastery. Many high-altitude communities in Haa and Sephu maintain lhakhangs associated with local protective deities and ancestral lineages.

Genyen

gen-yen

Local protective spirits or earth-lords — powerful territorial deities tied to specific mountains, valleys, and villages in Bhutan. Unlike universal Buddhist deities, genyen are localised and require community-specific rituals and offerings.

Tshechu Thongdroel

tshe-chu tong-droel

A large sacred thangka (scroll painting) — typically depicting Guru Rinpoche — unfurled at dawn on the final day of a Tsechu festival. Viewing the Thongdroel is believed to grant liberation from the cycle of rebirth.
